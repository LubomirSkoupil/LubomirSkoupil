Tools

  • Stable and precise PnL is at the heart of front office and middle office processes and controls. During my time at Ezpada I had the opportunity to work on several concepts of various types of PnL including energy products (standard profiles, shapes, transmission rights, multicurrency portfolios), FX and standardized exchange-traded commodity contracts.

    In 2015 I designed significant part of the PnL system Ezpada uses to this day - the system was built on reliable forward curves (one curve per grid, hourly granularity for power and gas), PnL calculated on position rather than individual tickets and on Total-to-Day PnL differences.

    In parallel to the official PnL implemented by IT I created several SQL and Python tools helping us explain and troubleshoot the official PnL (for example tool showing traders positions carried from one day to another and detail of PnL the positions generated between these days).

  • Despite its many problems VaR enables Risk Managers and Management to see the riskiness of the portfolio condensed into a single figure, that can be easily interpreted even by non-technical users.

    In Ezpada I created one simple VaR model (historical, linear positions only, excluding current month positions) and later I cooperated on creating (supplying most of the SQL functions for positions and prices) and design of much more complex VaR model (Monte Carlo, volatility weighted, all positions from Day Ahead, including option portfolio).

    I also have a lot of experience of reconciliating the VaR model results with Trading's expectations and helping Trading keep their exposure within the defined set of limits.

  • Reliable forward curves (FWC) with hourly granularity are a must-have for a well performing PnL and other Risk processes (credit, volatility and correlation estimation and modelling, cash flow projections).

    I cooperated with IT on implementation of FWC for power and gas grids and introduced several stabilizing concepts into the algorithm:

    • price source prioritization (example: "CZ: Trayport > PXE > brokers"; this way if Trayport marking fails and PXE does not publish prices, broker marks would be used for calculation and only small adjustment, if any, will be needed)
    • discarding the least liquid product prices in case of conflict (in March there will sometimes be quotes for Q2, Apr, May and June contracts, where the weighted average of monthly quotes does not equal quote for Q2, which creates confusion; this gest solved by disregarding the least liquid product quote - in this instance June)
    • including seasonality: this increases precision of the curves as it decreases valuation jumps for products that are becoming more liquid

  • Cash Flow projection is an important tool, especially for small companies trading with commodities with physical delivery - seeing potential problems well ahead of time gives Treasury and Risk departments enough flexibility to deal with the issues without unnecessary stress and cost.

    I created a Cash Flow report including projections of payments for commodities with physical and financial delivery (OTC and exchanges) and payments of taxes and fees that are settled periodically.

  • There are many problems with marking on energy markets:

    • illiquid products
    • energy is heavily traded in spreads - especially location and time spreads are widely popular
    • external sources often mark at different times causing high volatility of PnL and exaggerated VaR in portfolios including products marked based on different sources

    To avoid these problems, I developed and implemented an algorithm marking automatically based on real-time Trayport data (bids, offers, concluded trades on outright products). The algorithm allowed for marking on spread - the "trailing" market would follow the "base" market unless the trailing mark would get outside of the best bid / offer range of the trailing product, in which case the marked spread would either widen or narrow.
